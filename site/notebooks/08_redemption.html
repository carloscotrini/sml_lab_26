Regression Discontinuity: The Scholarship Cutoff

A university awards scholarships to students who score above 70 on an entrance exam. Students just below the cutoff are nearly identical to students just above it -- the only difference is the scholarship. This "sharp cutoff" creates a natural experiment.

We generate data where test scores smoothly predict future earnings, plus a genuine causal jump of $5,000 at the cutoff from the scholarship.

Verdict CREDIBLE

In Israel, a rule dating back to the philosopher Maimonides caps class sizes at 40 students. When enrollment hits 41, the school must split into two classes of about 20. Angrist and Lavy (1999) exploited this discontinuity to estimate the causal effect of class size on student achievement.

Schools with 40 students have one class of 40. Schools with 41 students suddenly have two classes of ~20. The students on either side of this enrollment cutoff are essentially identical -- but their class sizes differ dramatically.

The result: smaller classes significantly improved test scores. This finding would have been impossible to establish cleanly from observational data alone, since richer schools tend to have both smaller classes and higher-achieving students (confounding).

Simulated illustration of the Maimonides discontinuity. At enrollment = 40, class size drops sharply.
