      Repair
      
Instrumental Variables to the Rescue

      
An instrument \(Z\) (e.g., mortgage rate variation) affects \(X_1\) but has no direct effect on \(Y\) except through \(X_1\).
        The IV (two-stage least squares) estimator uses only the exogenous variation in \(X_1\) induced by \(Z\).

IV Bias

          

            How IV works:

            Stage 1: Regress \(\log(X_1)\) on \(Z\) to get predicted \(\widehat{\log(X_1)}\).

            Stage 2: Regress \(Y\) on \(\widehat{\log(X_1)}\).

            The Wald estimator: \(\hat{\beta}_{IV} = \frac{Cov(Y, Z)}{Cov(\log X_1, Z)}\)


            Requirements: (1) Relevance: \(Z\) must affect \(X_1\). (2) Exclusion: \(Z\) must not directly affect \(Y\).

First-Stage F-stat

          

            Rule of thumb: first-stage F-statistic should exceed 10.
            Below that threshold, IV estimates are biased toward OLS and confidence intervals are unreliable.
            A bad instrument can be worse than no instrument at all.
          

        

      

    


    
    
    
    

      
      

        

          Naive OLS shows a positive correlation between police numbers and crime rates: cities with more police have more crime.
          Does hiring police cause crime? Of course not — cities deploy more police because they have more crime (reverse causality).
        

        

          Levitt (1997) used electoral cycles as an instrument: politicians hire more police before elections regardless of crime trends.
          Using this exogenous variation, the IV estimate shows that more police reduces crime — a complete sign flip.
