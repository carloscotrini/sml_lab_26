The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem

The correct answer is B (and C is close, but B gives the precise mechanical intuition). The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell (FWL) theorem tells us exactly what "controlling for" means: it is a two-step residualization procedure.

Step 1: Regress \(Y\) on the controls (\(X_2\)), save residuals \(e_Y\).
...
Step 2: Regress \(X_1\) on the controls (\(X_2\)), save residuals \(e_{X_1}\).
...
Step 3: Regress \(e_Y\) on \(e_{X_1}\). The coefficient = the multiple regression coefficient on \(X_1\).

Partial Regression Plot (updates with controls)

Consider the regression \(Y = X_1 \beta_1 + X_2 \beta_2 + \varepsilon\). The FWL theorem states:

\[\hat{\beta}_1 = (M_2 X_1)' (M_2 X_1))^{-1} (M_2 X_1)' (M_2 Y)\]

where \(M_2 = I - X_2(X_2'X_2)^{-1}X_2'\) is the residual-maker matrix for \(X_2\). In other words, \(\hat{\beta}_1\) from the full regression equals the coefficient from regressing the residuals of \(Y\) on \(X_2\) against the residuals of \(X_1\) on \(X_2\).

Why this matters: "Controlling for \(X_2\)" literally means "removing the linear influence of \(X_2\) from both sides of the equation, then looking at what remains." The partial regression plot visualizes exactly this.

Danger of bad controls: When you include \(X_3 = 0.9Y + \eta_3\), the residual-maker \(M_3\) removes most of the variation in \(Y\), leaving only noise. The coefficient on \(X_1\) collapses toward zero -- not because \(X_1\) does not matter, but because you controlled away the outcome.
