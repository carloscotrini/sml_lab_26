For decades, observational studies suggested that hormone replacement therapy (HRT) reduced heart disease risk in postmenopausal women by ~40%. Millions were prescribed HRT.

Then the Women's Health Initiative (a large RCT) found the opposite: HRT increased heart disease risk by ~29%. The observational estimate had the wrong sign.

Why? Women who chose HRT were systematically healthier, wealthier, and more health-conscious. The "healthy user bias" -- an unobserved confounder -- overwhelmed the treatment effect. No amount of regression controls captured this.

This is the same pattern as our simulated job training program: selection bias flipped the sign of the estimated effect. The lesson: when treatment is not randomly assigned, regression may tell you the opposite of the truth.

"Is regression hopeless? No. You just need the right design."
