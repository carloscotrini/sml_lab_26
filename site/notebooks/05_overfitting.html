What went wrong?

The degree-12 polynomial memorized training noise. Its wiggly curve (blue) passes near every training point but diverges wildly between them. The true relationship (gold) is smooth: \(Y \approx 50 + 8\log(X_1)\). No polynomial can capture the log shape without overfitting.

The log-linear model captures the true functional form with just two parameters, outperforming every polynomial. Choosing the right model family matters more than tuning complexity.

In practice, use cross-validation: split data into K folds, train on K-1, test on the held-out fold, and average. This gives an honest estimate of out-of-sample performance.

Limit

Even Cross-Validation Can Overfit

What if you try 100 model specifications and pick the best CV score? The more models you search, the more likely you find one that looks good by chance.

This is model selection overfitting. Even honest CV scores become optimistic when you cherry-pick the best among many. Remedies: hold out a final test set that is never used for selection, or use statistical corrections for multiple comparisons.

In 1998, Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) — staffed by Nobel laureates — lost $4.6 billion in four months. Their models were fit to decades of historical bond spreads and converged beautifully on past data. When the Russian debt crisis created patterns outside the training distribution, the models catastrophically failed.
