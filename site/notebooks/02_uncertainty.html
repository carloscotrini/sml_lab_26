Beat 3 — Formalize

What Went Wrong: The Variance Formula

Classical OLS assumes homoscedastic errors: \(\text{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma^2\) for all \(i\). Under this assumption, the covariance of \(\hat\beta\) simplifies beautifully. But when variance depends on \(X\), you need the sandwich.

Classical (Assumes Homoscedasticity)

\[\text{Var}(\hat\beta) = \sigma^2 (X^\top X)^{-1}\]

Estimates one \(\hat\sigma^2\) for all observations. If variance actually grows with \(X_1\), this is wrong.

Sandwich / Robust (HC1 / White)

\[\text{Var}(\hat\beta) = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top \hat\Omega X (X^\top X)^{-1}\]

Where \(\hat\Omega = \text{diag}(\hat e_i^2)\) uses each residual individually. Scaled by \(\frac{n}{n-k}\) for HC1.

Beat 6 — Limit

The Small-Sample Trap

Robust SEs are a large-sample fix. With small \(n\), the sandwich estimator is biased, and coverage can be worse than classical. Find the crossover point.

At very small n (e.g., 20), the sandwich estimator underestimates variance because \(\hat e_i^2\) is noisy. HC1's \(\frac{n}{n-k}\) correction helps, but not enough.

Rule of thumb: use robust SEs when \(n \gtrsim 50\).

In 2008, Google Flu Trends claimed to predict flu outbreaks from search queries with impressive precision. The confidence intervals were extremely tight — but the team ignored autocorrelation in the errors.

When errors are correlated over time (\(\rho > 0\)), the effective sample size is much smaller than the nominal \(n\):

\[n_{\text{eff}} = n \cdot \frac{1 - \rho}{1 + \rho}\]

With \(\rho = 0.8\) and \(n = 100\):

\(n_{\text{eff}} = 100 \times \frac{0.2}{1.8} \approx 11\)

Your 100 observations carry the information of only 11 independent ones. The CIs should be roughly \(\sqrt{100/11} \approx 3\times\) wider.

Blue: nominal CI width. Red: CI width corrected for autocorrelation. The further \(\rho\) is from 0, the bigger the gap.
