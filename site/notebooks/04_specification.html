Repair

Use the Right Functional Form

Toggle between the linear model and the log-transformed model. The log model extrapolates much more gracefully because it respects diminishing returns.

Diminishing returns: Each additional square foot of living area adds less to the sale price. A 500 sqft apartment gaining 100 sqft is transformative; a 5,000 sqft mansion gaining 100 sqft is barely noticeable. The logarithm captures this naturally.
Limit

Even the Log Model Has Limits

Push \(X_1\) to extreme values. At \(X_1 = 100\) (100,000 sqft), even the log model's prediction becomes unreliable. Every functional form is a local approximation.

Every functional form is a local approximation. The log model is better than linear, but at \(X_1 = 100\) it still predicts with wild uncertainty. No model can reliably predict far outside its training data.

Before the 1986 Challenger launch, engineers examined O-ring damage only for flights where damage occurred. They saw no clear temperature trend and cleared the launch at 31°F.

Had they included all flights—including those with no damage at warm temperatures—the negative relationship between temperature and O-ring failure was obvious.

Red points: flights with O-ring damage. Blue points: flights with no damage (excluded from the original analysis). The trend is unmistakable when you include the full data. This is selection on the dependent variable—a specification error that cost seven lives.
