The Reveal: What You Couldn't See

Each dot below is a home sale. Color encodes neighborhood desirability (U) — the variable you omitted.

Desirable neighborhoods have both larger homes and higher prices. When you omit desirability, its effect gets absorbed into the living_area coefficient.

The Omitted Variable Bias formula:

$$E[\hat{\beta}_1] = \beta_1 + \underbrace{\beta_U \cdot \delta_1}_{\text{bias}}$$

where $\delta_1$ is the coefficient from regressing $U$ on $X_1$ (the auxiliary regression), and $\beta_U$ is the true effect of $U$ on $Y$.

Since $\beta_U > 0$ (desirability raises price) and $\delta_1 > 0$ (desirability is correlated with larger living area), the bias is positive.

Real-World Disaster: The Hormone Therapy That Killed

▶

For decades, observational studies suggested that Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) reduced heart disease risk in postmenopausal women by 30-50%. Millions of women were prescribed HRT based on these findings.

In 2002, the Women's Health Initiative (WHI) randomized controlled trial was stopped early because HRT was found to increase heart disease risk by 29%, along with higher breast cancer risk.

The culprit: Omitted Variable Bias. Women who chose HRT tended to be wealthier, more health-conscious, have better diets, exercise more, and have better access to healthcare. These confounders were positively correlated with both HRT use and better health outcomes.

The biased coefficient suggested a benefit when the true causal effect was harmful. Policy based on this estimate may have contributed to thousands of preventable heart attacks and cancers.

$$E[\hat{\beta}_{HRT}] = \underbrace{\beta_{HRT}}_{\text{true (positive, harmful)}} + \underbrace{\beta_{health} \cdot \delta_{HRT}}_{\text{large negative bias}}$$

Here $\beta_{health}$ is the (negative) effect of poor-health-behaviors on heart disease risk, and $\delta_{HRT}$ is the (negative) correlation between poor-health-behaviors and HRT uptake (healthier women were more likely to take HRT).

The product $\beta_{health} \cdot \delta_{HRT}$ is positive times negative = negative (or negative times negative = positive depending on coding). Either way, the bias was large enough to flip the sign of the estimated effect, making a harmful treatment appear protective.

A simplified Monte Carlo mirroring the HRT scenario: true causal effect is positive (harmful), but the omitted confounder creates enough negative bias to make the naive estimate negative (appears protective).
