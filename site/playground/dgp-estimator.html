Stage 1

Predicting Charlie, David, Evelyn

We've built the world. U pulls strings on the inputs, the inputs combine to make Y, and a noisy self-assessment X₃ rides along behind. Now: a new cohort arrives. Charlie, David, Evelyn. We have not seen their exam scores yet.

Charlie tells us he studied 2.5 hours and attended 11 lectures. David: 1.8 hours, 9 lectures. Evelyn: 3.2 hours, 12 lectures. We know each of their X₁ and X₂. We do not know their U (we never do). We do not know their ε (it hasn't happened yet).

What's our best guess for Charlie's score?

To answer that, we need a function — call it \( \hat{f} \) — that takes any (X₁, X₂) and returns a predicted Y. The simplest such function in the toolbox is a plane:

\( \hat{Y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 X_1 + \hat{\beta}_2 X_2 \)

Three numbers: an intercept and two slopes. Where do they come from? From data we've already seen — the previous cohort, n students whose X₁, X₂ and (crucially) Y we recorded. We pick the three numbers that make the plane sit as close as possible, in a least-squares sense, to all those past data points at once. That recipe is OLS: ordinary least squares.

Stage 2

The estimator arrives

Here is the magic trick at the heart of regression. We can't see U. We can't see ε. All we get is the cloud of past students.

OLS draws the plane that sits as close as possible to every dot at once: the plane that minimises the total squared distance from the dots to itself. The little grey sticks are like springs pulling on the plane; it settles where the total tension is smallest.

Now read off the predictions: stand the plane up at Charlie's (X₁, X₂) = (2.5, 11), look up to the plane, and that height is \( \hat{Y}_{\text{Charlie}} \). Same for David and Evelyn.

But our plane is straight, and the truth (the violet mesh) isn't. Look at the gap at the extremes. Going from 1 hour to 2 is life-changing. Going from 9 to 10 is just an extra hour of panic. Toggle "use log(X₁) as the feature" — the OLS plane bends to match.

This stage introduced:\( \hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top Y \) — OLS coefficients
Stage 3: the climax

The ghost in the residuals

So OLS gave us \( \hat{Y} \) for any new student. Done? Not quite. Look at where the plane misses. Each grey stick is a residual: the gap between the actual Y of a past student and what the plane predicted for them. By construction, residuals sum to zero. They should look like noise.

Here's something strange. Every semester we run, Alice (from page 1) sits above the plane. Every semester, Bob sits below. If the residuals were really just noise, they'd flip sign half the time.

They don't. Something is systematically lifting Alice and sinking Bob, and it's the thing we named at the very start. U.

Before you click, look at the residual sticks. They look grey, random, like noise. Now click Reveal — we'll recolour each stick by the U value of the student it belongs to.

Every positive residual is amber (high U). Every negative residual is teal (low U). The "noise" was never noise. It was self-discipline, invisibly lifting and lowering each point.

The ghost has a name. It's self-discipline.
The noise wasn't noise. It was someone we weren't looking at.

This is what a confounder does. It shapes the inputs. It shapes the outputs. When we leave it out of our model, the model happily blames the inputs for what the confounder did. Charlie's predicted score? It's also off by however much U pushes him. We just can't tell which way, because we don't see U.
