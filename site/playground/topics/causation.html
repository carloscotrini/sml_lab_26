Causation & instrumental variables

When X correlates with the error, OLS stays biased forever. IV finds a clean side-door.

Suppose we study the effect of weekly study hours \(X_1\) on exam score \(Y\). Students who study more tend to be more self-disciplined — call this hidden trait \(U\) — and self-discipline also raises exam scores directly (better sleep, more focus). OLS can't tell these apart. Pouring in more data doesn't help: the bias is baked into the estimator.

The way out: find a variable \(Z\) that wiggles \(X_1\) around but has no other path to \(Y\). A roommate's study hours work, if roommates are assigned at random: a studious roommate nudges you to study more (peer effect), but your roommate's habits don't directly change your exam score. That's an instrument.

The instrumental-variables estimator (simple one-regressor case):

\[ \hat\beta_{\text{IV}} \;=\; (Z^{\top} X)^{-1} Z^{\top} Y \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(Z, Y)}{\operatorname{Cov}(Z, X)}. \]

The causal diagram

A valid instrument \(Z\) must satisfy two conditions:

Relevance. \(Z\) moves \(X_1\). The green arrow \(Z \to X_1\) must exist and be strong enough to test against.
Exclusion. No other path from \(Z\) to \(Y\). \(Z\) must not arrow directly into \(Y\), and \(Z\) must not share a confounder with \(Y\) (i.e.\ \(U \not\to Z\)).

Random roommate assignment buys exclusion: your roommate's discipline is uncorrelated with yours.

Both estimators fit \(Y = \alpha + \beta_1 \log X_1 + \text{error}\). The truth is \(\beta_1 = 8\). OLS drifts upward because discipline \(U\) pushes both \(X_1\) and \(Y\). IV recovers a number near 8 — the bias is the gap between blue and green.

The first correct coefficient

For the first time, we have a coefficient that equals the truth — not because we observed the confounder, but because we found a clever way around it. That's what instruments buy you: identification without omniscience.

A weak instrument is worse than no instrument

Your CI is wider than the (biased!) OLS CI — and the bias doesn't vanish in finite samples either: weak IV estimates are centred on the OLS bias, not on the truth. You've paid variance to buy an illusion.
