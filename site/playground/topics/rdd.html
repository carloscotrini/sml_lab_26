Regression discontinuity design

The sharp cutoff is the quietest randomiser in empirical economics.

Imagine a scholarship awarded to every student whose test score reaches 70. A student with 69 and a student with 70 are, by every measure we can think of, nearly the same person. But one gets the money and the other does not. If a year later their outcomes differ by a visible jump at the threshold, that jump cannot be explained by ability, motivation, or any other trait that varies smoothly across the cutoff. It must be the scholarship.

That is the logic of regression discontinuity. Near a sharp cutoff, units just above and just below are nearly identical — so the jump in outcomes at the cutoff identifies the causal effect of treatment, without any random assignment.

Formally: \[ \tau_{\text{RDD}} \;=\; \lim_{r \downarrow c} E[Y \mid R = r] \;-\; \lim_{r \uparrow c} E[Y \mid R = r] \] where \(R\) is the running variable (here, the test score) and \(c\) is the cutoff (here, 70).

Design validity checks

Before trusting that gap, an RDD analyst runs three diagnostics. Each one can kill the design.

a. McCrary density test

Distribution of the running variable near the cutoff. A spike at the threshold suggests students are gaming their score.

✓ Density smooth across cutoff — no manipulation detected.

b. Covariate balance

Pre-treatment covariates (age, prior GPA) should have similar means just above and just below the cutoff.

✓ Covariates balanced across the cutoff.

c. Placebo cutoff

Move the cutoff to a value where no treatment actually occurs. The "effect" should vanish.

RDD fails if agents manipulate the running variable. If students could retake exams until they hit 70, the groups just above and below are no longer comparable — motivated strivers pile up on the treated side. The density test is your canary.

Where RDD has paid off

  • Angrist & Lavy (1999) — Maimonides' Rule: Israeli class sizes are capped at 40, so a grade with 41 enrolled is split in two. The sharp drop in class size at each multiple of 40 identifies the causal effect of class size on test scores. QJE paper.
  • Carpenter & Dobkin (2009) — Minimum legal drinking age: US mortality jumps discontinuously on the 21st birthday. A clean estimate of how alcohol access affects death rates. AEJ: Applied.
  • Lee (2008) — US House incumbency: parties barely winning an election are, by luck of a few votes, nearly identical to those barely losing. The discontinuity at 50% vote share identifies the causal incumbent advantage. J. Econometrics.

Looking back

After eight sections of everything going wrong — omitted variables, heteroscedasticity, multicollinearity, endogeneity, measurement error, selection, post-treatment controls, colliders — RDD is the first design that earns OLS's trust back. Same estimator, different credibility. Not because the math changed, but because the design did the work the regression couldn't.

That is the lesson of the course. A coefficient means whatever the design says it means.
