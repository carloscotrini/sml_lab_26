Heteroscedasticity & robust standard errors

When the noise fans out with X, classical confidence intervals lie. Robust sandwich SEs patch the leak — but only when the sample is big enough.

Classical OLS standard errors assume that the noise \(\varepsilon\) has the same variance at every value of the regressors. In our exam-score DGP, that is not true: we built in \(\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 0.5\,|X_1|)\), meaning students who study more hours have more variable outcomes, not just higher average scores. The spread of points in a plot of \(Y\) against \(X_1\) should look like a funnel — narrow on the left, wider on the right.

Heteroscedasticity does not bias the OLS point estimate — the coefficient \(\hat{\beta}\) is still consistent. What breaks is the standard error. The usual formula assumes constant variance:

\[ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_{\text{classical}}(\hat{\beta}) = \hat{\sigma}^2 (X^\top X)^{-1}. \]

When the variance truly depends on \(X\), the right-hand side is wrong. The correct sampling variance follows the sandwich form

\[ \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}) = (X^\top X)^{-1}\, X^\top \Omega X\, (X^\top X)^{-1}, \]

where \(\Omega\) is the diagonal matrix of \(\varepsilon_i\)'s variances. The Huber–White (HC0) estimator replaces \(\Omega\) with \(\mathrm{diag}(\hat{\varepsilon}_i^2)\); HC1 rescales by \(n / (n - p)\). The result: heteroscedasticity-robust standard errors whose 95 % intervals actually cover \(\beta\) close to 95 % of the time.

Watch out: at small n the sandwich estimator is biased downward — robust SEs can undercover worse than classical SEs. The sandwich fix is only asymptotic; in tiny samples both methods are unreliable.

Takeaway

Heteroscedasticity is a standard-error problem, not a coefficient problem. Use robust SEs by default when you have a reasonable \(n\); in very small samples, neither classical nor HC1 is trustworthy and you should prefer bootstrap or HC3.
