Collider bias & bad controls

More controls are not always better. Sometimes adding one ruins the estimate.

An analyst's instinct — the more variables I control for, the cleaner the estimate — is wrong. A control that is itself caused by the outcome (or by the treatment) is a collider. Conditioning on it opens a spurious path between the other variables and distorts everything downstream.

The canonical picture in graph form: \[ A \longrightarrow C \longleftarrow B \] \(A\) and \(B\) are unrelated. But condition on \(C\) (slice the data by it, or include it as a regressor) and an association between \(A\) and \(B\) appears out of thin air.

SetupWhy \(X_3\) is a collider here

Before we touch any regression, let's pin down why \(X_3\) — the student's self-assessed performance — counts as a collider in our running exam DGP. The reasoning lives entirely in the structural equations; no data are needed.

The DGP writes \(X_3\) as \[ X_3 \;=\; 0.9\,Y \;+\; \eta_3. \] So \(Y\) causes \(X_3\), not the other way around. The student takes the exam, earns a score \(Y\), and then reports how well they think it went. Their self-assessment is downstream of the actual outcome.

Now look at \(Y\) itself. In the DGP, the score is produced by hours studied, class size, unobserved self-discipline, and noise: \[ Y \;=\; 50 + 8\log X_1 + 3X_2 + 6U + \varepsilon. \] That means \(Y\) has multiple arrows pointing in — from \(X_1\), \(X_2\), and \(U\) — and a single arrow pointing out, to \(X_3\).

A node is a collider on a path when the two arrows of the path both point into it. Consider the path \(X_1 \to Y \leftarrow U\): one arrow from \(X_1\) into \(Y\), one from \(U\) into \(Y\). Both heads land on \(Y\). So \(Y\) is a collider on this particular path, and the path is blocked by default — collider paths do not carry information until you condition on the collider (or on one of its descendants).

Here comes the twist. \(X_3\) is not the collider; \(Y\) is. But \(X_3\) is a descendant of the collider \(Y\) (\(Y \to X_3\)). A classical result from d-separation says: conditioning on a descendant of a collider has the same unblocking effect as conditioning on the collider itself. Including \(X_3\) as a regressor is, for path-blocking purposes, equivalent to (partially) peeking at \(Y\).

That is the precise reason controlling for \(X_3\) opens the otherwise-blocked path \(X_1 \to Y \leftarrow U\) and lets spurious dependence between \(X_1\) and \(U\) leak into the regression. The demo below makes it numerical.

Demo 1The exam DGP: what happens if you control for \(X_3\)?

Back to our running exam scenario. The true model is \(Y = 50 + 8\log X_1 + 3X_2 + 6U + \varepsilon\), and the analyst wants the causal effect of hours studied (\(X_1\)) on the exam score. There is a fourth, tempting variable: \(X_3\), the student's self-assessed performance, measured after the exam. Because \(X_3 = 0.9\,Y + \eta_3\), it is a downstream consequence of \(Y\) — a descendant of the collider \(Y\) on the back-door path from \(X_1\) through \(U\). Toggle it on and off.

Hover or check the box above — when \(X_3\) is conditioned on, a spurious path between \(X_1\) and \(U\) opens.

Reading the picture. The solid black arrows are the real causal arrows from the DGP: \(U \to X_1\), \(U \to Y\), \(X_1 \to Y\), \(X_2 \to Y\), and \(Y \to X_3\). There is no direct causal arrow between \(X_1\) and \(U\) beyond \(U \to X_1\), which is already drawn.

Box off. When we leave \(X_3\) alone, the path \(X_1 \to Y \leftarrow U\) is blocked because \(Y\) is a collider on it, and collider paths are blocked by default. No spurious information flows between \(X_1\) and \(U\) through \(Y\).

Box on. Ticking the box conditions on \(X_3\), a descendant of \(Y\). That unblocks the collider path: \(X_1 \to Y \leftarrow U\) is now open, and information flows along it, manufacturing a spurious statistical dependence between \(X_1\) and \(U\).

The red dashed arc is not a causal arrow. It depicts the spurious dependence the regression now sees between \(X_1\) and \(U\). With \(X_1\) and \(U\) no longer independent in the conditioned sample, the regression can no longer cleanly assign the variation in \(X_1\) to its causal coefficient on \(Y\). That contamination is exactly what shows up as the shrunken \(\hat\beta_1\) in the metrics panel.
