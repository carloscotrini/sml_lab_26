Specification error & extrapolation

When the shape of your model is wrong, predictions outside the data range go off the rails.

OLS doesn't know what shape the relationship between inputs and outputs should take. You hand it a design matrix — a list of features — and it finds the best linear combination. If the true relationship is curved and you fit a straight line, OLS still returns a best-fit line. That line is honest about its job: it minimises squared error over the distribution you trained on. The population-level OLS target is the linear projection

\[ \beta^\star \;=\; \arg\min_\beta \; \mathbb{E}\!\left[(Y - X\beta)^2\right]. \]

Nothing in that definition says \(\beta^\star\) is close to the truth. If the truth is \(Y = 50 + 8\log X_1 + 3 X_2 + 6U + \varepsilon\), the best linear-in-\(X_1\) approximation is still an approximation of a curve. Inside the data range, a straight line through a gentle log curve can be indistinguishable to the eye. Outside — where you didn't see any data — the two diverge.

Fit linear to a log truth and your predictions are fine inside the data range and catastrophically wrong outside it. That's specification error, and extrapolation is where it bites.

3. Residual diagnostic

If your model is correctly specified, residuals should look like random noise around zero. If they show a pattern, the model is missing structure. Look closely at the linear residuals.

Linear residuals (vs \(X_1\))
Log residuals (vs \(X_1\))

The red trend line on the linear residuals traces a U-shape: positive at the extremes, negative in the middle. That's the textbook fingerprint of a missing nonlinearity. Log residuals look like a shapeless cloud — exactly what you want.
