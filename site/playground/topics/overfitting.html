Overfitting & the bias–variance tradeoff

A model that fits training data perfectly predicts new data terribly.

That sentence isn't a paradox — it's the difference between memorising and learning. A flexible enough model can thread a curve through every training point. But the training points carry noise, and bending the curve to catch that noise bakes the noise into the model. When a new point arrives, the memorised noise is simply wrong.

The classic decomposition of expected squared prediction error at a point \(x\) makes this trade precise:

\[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[(Y - \hat{f}(x))^2\right] \;=\; \underbrace{\bigl(\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)] - f(x)\bigr)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\hat{f}(x)\bigr)}_{\text{Variance}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible}} \]

A simple model (low-degree polynomial) has high bias but low variance: it misses the shape, but different samples give nearly the same wrong answer. A complex model (high-degree polynomial) has low bias but high variance: it can capture the shape, but it also chases the noise, so different samples give wildly different fits. The sweet spot lives in between.

4. The search-breadth trap

Cross-validation is supposed to protect us — pick the model that wins on held-out folds. But if you try enough specifications, one of them wins by luck. Below, we draw a single dataset, run 5-fold CV on the first \(k\) randomly generated specifications, and report the best CV R² and its honest test R² on a fresh hold-out. As \(k\) grows, the CV R² inflates while the test R² stagnates or drops.

Even cross-validation can overfit — if you search the space hard enough, the noise in the validation folds starts looking like signal. The cure is a truly held-out test set, pre-registered model choices, or a penalty that grows with search breadth.
