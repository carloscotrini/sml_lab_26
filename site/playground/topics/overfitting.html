Overfitting & the bias–variance tradeoff

A model that fits training data perfectly predicts new data terribly.

That sentence isn't a paradox — it's the difference between memorising and learning. A flexible enough model can thread a curve through every training point. But the training points carry noise, and bending the curve to catch that noise bakes the noise into the model. When a new point arrives, the memorised noise is simply wrong.

The classic decomposition of expected squared prediction error at a point \(x\) makes this trade precise:

\[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[(Y - \hat{f}(x))^2\right] \;=\; \underbrace{\bigl(\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)] - f(x)\bigr)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}\bigl(\hat{f}(x)\bigr)}_{\text{Variance}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible}} \]

A simple model (low-degree polynomial) has high bias but low variance: it misses the shape, but different samples give nearly the same wrong answer. A complex model (high-degree polynomial) has low bias but high variance: it can capture the shape, but it also chases the noise, so different samples give wildly different fits. The sweet spot lives in between.

The data-generating process we use throughout the playground is \( Y = 50 + 8\log(X_1) + 3X_2 + 6U + \varepsilon \). Below we ignore \(X_2\) and \(U\) and fit polynomials in \(X_1\) of increasing degree, splitting the sample 80/20 into training and test.

3. Right model family beats right complexity

The polynomials in the previous two panels could even compete with the truth only because of the universal approximation theorem (Stone–Weierstrass): on a bounded interval, any continuous function — including \(\log\) — can be matched to any desired accuracy by a polynomial of sufficient degree. That's why pushing the slider right gives an arbitrarily flexible fit on the training data.

But universal approximation comes at a price in data. The higher the degree, the more parameters you need to pin down, and the more variance every noisy training point injects into the coefficient estimates. Fitting \(Y = a + b\log X_1\) directly uses the correct functional form — two parameters instead of nine, zero extra variance paid for chasing the shape.

4. The search-breadth trap

Cross-validation is supposed to protect us — pick the model whose predictions hold up on held-out folds. But if you try enough specifications, one of them wins by luck. Below we fit many candidate models to a single 210-student dataset and ask: does the best CV R² we can find really reflect the best model, or just the one that got lucky?

How a "specification" is built. Each candidate is a small random subset of features drawn from a pool of thirteen: the real signal columns (\(X_1\), \(\log X_1\), \(X_1^2\), \(X_2\), \(X_2^2\), \(X_1 X_2\), \(X_3\)) plus six columns of pure random noise that carry no information about \(Y\) at all. For each candidate we randomly pick 1 to 5 of these thirteen features and regress \(Y\) on them. The pool deliberately mixes signal and noise so that — by chance — a specification can "win" on CV simply because its noise columns happened to correlate with validation-fold fluctuations. That is the luck we want to make visible.

The slider picks how many of these random specifications we try. We report the best CV R² among the first \(k\) we looked at (green) and the honest test R² of that "winning" specification, measured on a fresh hold-out the search never saw (gold). As \(k\) grows the green line creeps up — the winner keeps winning — while the gold line stays flat or drops. That gap is the cost of searching too hard.

Even cross-validation can overfit — if you search the space hard enough, the noise in the validation folds starts looking like signal. The cure is a truly held-out test set, pre-registered model choices, or a penalty that grows with search breadth.
