Omitted variable bias

When a hidden cause of Y distorts the coefficient you see.

Suppose exam scores \(Y\) depend on hours studied \(X_1\), lectures attended \(X_2\), and an unobserved trait \(U\) (self-discipline): \(Y = 50 + 8\log X_1 + 3 X_2 + 2U + \varepsilon\). Because disciplined students also study more, \(U\) and \(X_1\) are correlated. If we regress \(Y\) on \(X_1\) alone, the fitted coefficient absorbs some of \(U\)'s effect and no longer estimates the causal impact of studying.

The algebra is tidy. If the true model is \(Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 U + \varepsilon\) but we fit only \(X_1\), then \[ \hat\beta_1^{\text{short}} \;=\; \beta_1 \;+\; \beta_2 \cdot \delta_1 \] where \(\delta_1\) is the slope from regressing the omitted \(U\) on \(X_1\). The bias is the product of how much U matters for Y and how much U tracks X₁. Zero out either factor and the bias vanishes.

Simpson's paradox: sign-flip demo

A second DGP to make the point brutally: severity \(U\) causes both a treatment-like \(X\) and the outcome \(Y\), and the true effect of \(X\) on \(Y\) is negative. Ignore \(U\) and the fitted slope is positive; control for it and the sign flips back. Drag the slider to dial in how much of \(U\) the model gets to see.
