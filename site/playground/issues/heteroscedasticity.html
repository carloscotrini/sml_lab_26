The funnel of false confidence

Dr. Gauss fit a line through hours studied and exam scores. The line is not the problem. The spread around it is.

Section 1

The funnel

Dr. Gauss fit Y ~ X1 — exam score on hours studied — and declared the job done. Look carefully at the residuals. Students who studied a lot cluster tightly around the line. Students who barely studied scatter wildly: some got lucky, some blanked out, some partly-knew the material. The noise is not uniform — it shrinks as study hours grow.

This is the shape of drunk darts: prepared students throw at the bullseye and cluster tight; students running on three hours of study (and are guessing, half-remembering, or freezing in the exam hall) throw all over the wall. The aim is unbiased. The spread is not constant.

observed students residual (vertical stick) OLS fitted line
Definition. This is called heteroscedasticity: non-constant variance. Formally, \(\mathrm{Var}(\varepsilon_i \mid X_i) = \sigma_i^2\) with \(\sigma_i^2\) not constant across observations.
Section 2

The 95% that isn't

The classical 95% confidence interval on \(\hat{\beta}_1\) assumes constant variance. If the variance actually grows with X1, the classical CI is too narrow in some regions and too wide in others. Averaged across many samples, it has the wrong coverage.

A 95% CI is a promise about a procedure: if you run the whole experiment a hundred times, the interval should contain the truth about ninety-five times. Let us check Dr. Gauss's promise. Press the button, draw a hundred samples, and count.

(Robust SE is slightly anticonservative in finite samples — a known HC1 property that shrinks as \(n\) grows.)

Section 3

How bad is bad?

The DGP has a dial. The noise variance is \(\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon \mid X_1) = \sigma^2 \cdot X_1^{-h}\) — students who barely study end up with wildly scattered scores (a bad week, a lucky guess, a missed concept), while conscientious students land consistently high. When \(h = 0\), the variance is constant and the classical formula is fine. As \(h\) grows, the spread concentrates at small \(X_1\) — exactly where \(\log X_1\) has its highest leverage — and that's what makes the classical formula go wrong. Section 4

Does the data actually have it?

How do you know your regression has heteroscedasticity? The Breusch–Pagan test. Regress the squared residuals on the original regressors. If X explains the squared residuals — i.e. the error size varies with X — there is heteroscedasticity.

Under \(H_0\) (homoscedasticity) the test statistic \(nR^2\) follows \(\chi^2_p\) where \(p\) is the number of regressors (excluding the constant). If \(p\)-value \(< 0.05\), reject.

Section 5

The repair

The fix is embarrassingly simple: use robust standard errors (HC1, sometimes called sandwich or Huber–White–Eicker). Keep the coefficient estimates exactly as OLS gave them. Change the variance formula to

\[ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat{\beta}) \;=\; (X^\top X)^{-1} \left(\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\varepsilon}_i^{\,2}\; x_i x_i^\top\right) (X^\top X)^{-1}. \]
The bread is \((X^\top X)^{-1}\). The meat is \(\sum \hat{\varepsilon}_i^2 x_i x_i^\top\). Each observation contributes its own squared residual — no constant-variance assumption needed.
The coefficient is the same. Only the uncertainty changes.
Section 6

Small-sample caveat

Robust SEs are asymptotic. They work in the limit. In small samples each \(\hat{\varepsilon}_i^2\) is itself a noisy estimate of \(\sigma_i^2\), and with only 20 or 30 observations that noise can make the sandwich estimator worse than the classical one. The repair has a minimum-sample-size cost.
