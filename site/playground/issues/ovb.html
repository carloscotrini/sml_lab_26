The setup

Dr. Gauss's lucky coefficient

On the last page, Dr. Gauss fit a single regression:

$$ Y \;=\; \beta_0 \;+\; \beta_2\, X_2 \;+\; \text{error} $$

He was delighted. His estimate came out near β̂2 ≈ 3.1, which looked suspiciously close to the truth (β2 = 3). Case closed?

Not quite. Dr. Gauss made a mistake the DGP quietly punishes: he ignored U, the hidden self-discipline. The fact that his answer landed near 3 is a coincidence of the numbers in this particular DGP — the bias is small here, but it is real, it is signable, and in a nastier world it would bite him.

Let's look under the hood.

The OVB formula

Where the ghost enters

The true DGP for exam scores is

$$ Y \;=\; 50 \;+\; 8\,\log X_1 \;+\; 3\,X_2 \;+\; 2\,U \;+\; \varepsilon, $$

and both X2 (lectures attended) and U (self-discipline) are positively correlated, because disciplined students attend more lectures. When you regress Y on X2 alone, your estimator converges not to β2 but to

$$ \operatorname{plim}\hat\beta_2^{\text{short}} \;=\; \beta_2 \;+\; \beta_U \cdot \delta_U, \qquad \delta_U \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X_2,\, U)}{\operatorname{Var}(X_2)}. $$

The bias is βU · δU. In this DGP, βU = 2 (self-discipline helps scores) and δU > 0 (self-discipline drives lecture attendance upward). Both are positive, so Dr. Gauss's estimate is too high. The ghost of self-discipline is silently padding the coefficient on lectures.

When γ2 = 0, X2 and U become uncorrelated, δU collapses to zero, and the bias vanishes. The short regression is only safe when the confounder doesn't actually confound.

The repair

Include U, and the bias evaporates

In the short fit, the coefficient on X2 absorbs βU·δU. In the full fit, once U is an explicit column in the design matrix, there is nothing left for X2 to steal. The estimate lands on 3.

But this repair only works because we can observe U. In real life, self-discipline is hidden, childhood nutrition is unrecorded, genetic predisposition is invisible. When the confounder is unobservable, we cannot just drop it into the regression — we need heavier machinery: proxy variables (use something correlated with U, like GPA), or instrumental variables (find a source of variation in X2 that is independent of U, like a random assignment to attend lectures). Both partially rescue us. Neither is free.

When OVB goes nuclear

Here the bias was around +0.3 — big enough to worry about, small enough that Dr. Gauss slept through it. But OVB has a more spectacular mode: it can flip the sign of a coefficient, turning a positive effect into a negative one. For the most dramatic version — and the story of the 1973 Berkeley admissions lawsuit — see the Simpson's paradox page.
