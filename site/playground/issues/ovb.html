The setup

Dr. Gauss's suspiciously lucky coefficient

On the last page, Dr. Gauss fit a single regression:

$$ Y \;=\; \beta_0 \;+\; \beta_2\, X_2 \;+\; \text{error} $$

He was delighted. His estimate came out near \(\hat\beta_2 \approx 3.1\), which looked suspiciously close to the truth (\(\beta_2 = 3\)). Case closed?

Not quite. Dr. Gauss was lucky. The DGP does produce bias on his estimate — but in this particular scenario, the bias happens to be small (around +0.5 in expectation). A single noisy sample puts him near 3, and he never notices the ghost. That's the real danger of OVB: sometimes it's loud enough to flip a sign, and sometimes it's quiet enough to let you believe a wrong answer.

This page does three things. First we derive the OVB formula and show that, yes, the bias in Dr. Gauss's DGP is real — just small. Then we build a different DGP (a gym / health-score scenario) where the very same mechanism produces a bias so large you can't miss it. Finally, we confront the hard question: how do you detect OVB in practice, when you can't see the confounder?

The OVB formula

Where the ghost enters

The true DGP for exam scores is

$$ Y \;=\; 50 \;+\; 8\,\log X_1 \;+\; 3\,X_2 \;+\; 2\,U \;+\; \varepsilon, $$

and both \(X_2\) (lectures attended) and \(U\) (self-discipline) are positively correlated, because disciplined students attend more lectures. When you regress \(Y\) on \(X_2\) alone, your estimator converges not to \(\beta_2\) but to

\[ \operatorname{plim}\hat\beta_2^{\text{short}} \;=\; \beta_2 \;+\; \beta_U \cdot \delta_U, \qquad \delta_U \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X_2,\, U)}{\operatorname{Var}(X_2)}. \]

The bias is \(\beta_U \cdot \delta_U\). In Dr. Gauss's DGP, \(\beta_U = 2\) and \(\gamma_2 = 0.3\), so \(\delta_U = 0.3 / (0.3^2 + 1) \approx 0.275\), and the bias is +0.55. Positive — Dr. Gauss overshoots — but small enough that on a single noisy sample with \(n = 150\) he can land near 3.0 and think all is well.

Key point. OVB is real in Dr. Gauss's DGP. Its magnitude, however, is modest. The magnitude depends on two things: how strongly \(U\) drives \(X_2\) (the slope \(\gamma_2\)) and how strongly \(U\) drives \(Y\) (the coefficient \(\beta_U\)). Push either of them up and the bias explodes.

How the right-hand plot is computed — the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell construction. The right-hand scatter is not a raw \(Y\) vs \(X_2\) plot. It is built in three steps, which together isolate the part of \(X_2\)'s effect on \(Y\) that is not already explained by \(\log X_1\) and \(U\):
  1. Step 1. Regress \(Y\) on \((\log X_1,\, U)\); keep the residuals \(r_Y\). These are "the part of \(Y\) not explained by \(X_1\) and \(U\)."
  2. Step 2. Regress \(X_2\) on \((\log X_1,\, U)\); keep the residuals \(r_{X_2}\). These are "the part of \(X_2\) not explained by \(X_1\) and \(U\)."
  3. Step 3. Scatter \(r_Y\) against \(r_{X_2}\). Each dot is one student's pair \((r_{X_2,i},\, r_{Y,i})\). Fit a line through this cloud.
The slope of that line equals \(\hat\beta_2\) from the full multivariate regression \(Y \sim \log(X_1) + X_2 + U\) — exactly. This is the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem (same procedure derived in the FWL section of the lecture notes): running a multivariate regression is equivalent to partialling out the other covariates from both sides and then running a single simple regression on the residuals.

A second scenario — where OVB bites hard

Dr. Watson's gym study

To see OVB rather than argue it, let's switch dataset. Dr. Watson runs an observational study on 200 office workers. He records:

  • X — hours per week at the gym
  • Y — a composite health score (blood pressure, resting heart rate, VO2-max, etc.)

He does not observe \(U\) — each worker's baseline fitness, their athletic background before the study began. But baseline fitness drives both: fitter people go to the gym more often and have higher health scores for reasons independent of the gym.

\[ U \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), \quad X = 2 + 1.2\,U + \eta, \quad \eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.4), \] \[ Y = 3\,X + 6\,U + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1). \]

The true causal effect of gym hours on health is \(\beta = 3\). What does Dr. Watson's short regression \(Y\) on \(X\) recover?

Plim calculation. \(\delta_U = \operatorname{Cov}(X, U) / \operatorname{Var}(X) = 1.2 / (1.2^2 \cdot 1 + 0.4) = 1.2 / 1.84 \approx 0.652\). Bias \(= \beta_U \cdot \delta_U = 6 \cdot 0.652 \approx\) +3.91. So \(\operatorname{plim}\hat\beta^{\text{short}} \approx 3.00 + 3.91 =\) 6.91 — more than double the truth.

Read the picture. The histogram is centered near 6.91, not 3.00. The truth is entirely outside the sampling distribution of \(\hat\beta^{\text{short}}\). No amount of data cures this — doubling \(n\) would just concentrate the histogram tighter around 6.91. OVB is not noise; it's a systematic shift.
The contrast. In Dr. Gauss's studying DGP, \(\gamma_2 = 0.3\) and \(\beta_U = 2\), so the bias is only +0.55. In Dr. Watson's gym DGP, \(\gamma = 1.2\) and \(\beta_U = 6\), so the bias is +3.91. Both scenarios suffer from OVB. Only one gives you a coefficient that screams "wrong" when you look at it. The mechanism is identical; the magnitude depends entirely on the DGP.
The repair — when we can see U

Include U (correctly specified), and the bias evaporates

If we are lucky enough to observe the confounder (a rare luxury), we can simply include it — but we must specify the model to match the DGP. Recall that the true DGP uses \(\log X_1\), not \(X_1\) linearly. If we fit \(Y\) on \(X_1, X_2, U\) with a linear \(X_1\) term, the model is still misspecified and \(\hat\beta_2\) can be contaminated by the misspecification. The correctly specified repair is:

\[ Y \;=\; \beta_0 \;+\; \beta_1 \log(X_1) \;+\; \beta_2\, X_2 \;+\; \beta_U\, U \;+\; \varepsilon. \]

Fit \(Y\) on \(\log(X_1)\), \(X_2\), \(U\) (note: \(\log(X_1)\) — the DGP uses \(\log(X_1)\), not \(X_1\) linearly). With this correctly specified model and \(U\) observed, \(\hat\beta_2\) recovers the true \(\beta_2 \approx 3\):

In the short fit, the coefficient on \(X_2\) absorbs \(\beta_U \cdot \delta_U\). In the full fit — once \(\log(X_1)\) captures the nonlinear education term and \(U\) is an explicit column in the design matrix — there is nothing left for \(X_2\) to steal. The estimate lands on 3.

But this repair only works because we can observe \(U\). In real life, self-discipline is hidden, baseline fitness is unrecorded, genetic predisposition is invisible. So a natural question follows — and it is the question your instructor asked: can we even detect that our regression is suffering from OVB, when the confounder is unobservable?
Another way out: instrumental variables. If you cannot observe \(U\) but can find a variable \(Z\) that (a) correlates with \(X_2\), (b) affects \(Y\) only through \(X_2\), and (c) is unrelated to \(U\), then the IV estimator \[ \hat\beta_2^{\text{IV}} \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(Z,\, Y)}{\operatorname{Cov}(Z,\, X_2)} \] is consistent for \(\beta_2\) even when \(U\) is unobservable. We explore this in detail on the Causation page — see "The Chicken or the Egg" from the hub.
The honest question

How do you detect OVB in practice?

Your instinct — run a statistical test — is right, but the answer is painful. Let's start with the hard truth.

No direct test exists. There is no goodness-of-fit statistic, no \(p\)-value, and no residual diagnostic that can tell you "this single observational regression is omitting a confounder." The entire definition of OVB is that the confounder is unobserved — you cannot regress against a variable you don't have. Anyone who sells you a one-click OVB test is selling snake oil.

What we do have is a portfolio of heuristics, bounding arguments, and design-based workarounds. None is a silver bullet, but together they let a careful analyst shrink uncertainty about whether their coefficient is contaminated. Here are the five most useful.

  1. Coefficient stability across specifications

    Fit \(Y\) on \(X\) with no controls. Add one observed control. Add another. If \(\hat\beta\) keeps drifting as you pile on controls, you probably still have unmeasured confounding. If it settles and stops moving, you've plausibly captured the important confounders.

    Limit: stability is necessary but not sufficient. Two unobserved confounders pulling in opposite directions can leave \(\hat\beta\) artificially stable.

  2. Sensitivity analysis (Cinelli–Hazlett "robustness value")

    Ask: how strong would a hypothetical unobserved confounder need to be — measured by the partial \(R^2\) it shares with \(X\) and with \(Y\) — to fully explain away your result? This produces a single number, the robustness value (RV). A small RV (e.g. 1%) means a weak ghost can sink you; a large RV (e.g. 40%) means an unobserved confounder would have to be implausibly strong.

    Limit: you still need to argue in words that no such strong confounder plausibly exists. The math bounds; it doesn't prove.

  3. Proxy variables

    If you suspect a specific confounder \(U\), find anything observable that correlates with it. For the gym study: a short survey question about exercise before the study period. For the studying study: GPA as a proxy for discipline. Include the proxy; any reduction in \(|\hat\beta|\) is circumstantial evidence that a real confounder was lurking.

    Limit: an imperfect proxy only partially absorbs the bias. You've reduced OVB, not eliminated it.

  4. Instrumental variables (IV)

    Find an instrument \(Z\) that (a) shifts \(X\), (b) affects \(Y\) only through \(X\), and (c) is unrelated to \(U\). Two-stage least squares then gives a consistent estimate even with OVB. You compare \(\hat\beta^{\text{OLS}}\) to \(\hat\beta^{\text{IV}}\) — a large gap is strong evidence of OVB (this comparison is the Hausman test).

    Limit: finding a valid instrument is often harder than the original problem. Weak instruments produce huge variance. Invalid ones replace one bias with another.

  5. Natural or designed experiments (randomization, RDD, DiD)

    The gold standard. Randomly assign \(X\) — e.g. randomize who gets a gym membership voucher — and by construction \(U\) cannot correlate with \(X\). OVB is eliminated by design, not by statistical correction. Observational methods (IV, regression discontinuity, difference-in-differences) try to approximate this; real experiments deliver it.

    Limit: randomization is often infeasible (you cannot randomize smoking, parental income, or war) or unethical. Quasi-experiments require their own identifying assumptions.

In real studies you never have the luxury of knowing when \(\hat\beta\) has "arrived" at the truth — you only get to see that it stopped moving. If it keeps moving as you add more controls, you still have unmeasured confounding. If it stabilizes, you have circumstantial evidence (never proof) that the ghosts have been captured.
The meta-lesson. OVB cannot be tested away; it has to be designed away (experiment), identified away (valid instrument), or bounded away (sensitivity analysis + domain argument). Good empirical work doesn't prove OVB is absent; it convinces a skeptical reader that the remaining OVB is too small to matter.

When OVB goes nuclear

In the gym study, bias inflated the coefficient. But OVB has an even more spectacular mode: it can flip the sign of a coefficient, turning a positive effect into a negative one. For that dramatic version — and the story of the 1973 Berkeley admissions lawsuit — see the Simpson's paradox page.
