Issue 02 · Simpson's paradox

The Sign Flip

A hospital administrator thinks nursing hurts patients. The data says so. The data is correct. The interpretation would be a catastrophe.

1. The administrator's dilemma

A hospital administrator looks at the data. Patients who receive more nursing hours per day take longer to recover. She considers cutting nursing staff to "speed up recovery." A statistician walks in and says: wait.

mild (U=1)severe (U=10)

Emphasize: the fit is not wrong in a mathematical sense. The arithmetic is exact. The interpretation would be catastrophic.

"If we cut nursing by two hours a day, patients will recover two days faster, right?" — the administrator, extrapolating from \(\hat\beta = +1.44\).

2. The hidden variable

Every patient was admitted with some severity \(U\). For this toy example, half the patients are mild (\(U = 3\)) and half are severe (\(U = 8\)) — a clean two-point split that makes the stratification easy to see. The severely ill get more nursing (naturally), and they also take longer to recover (also naturally). Severity is doing the work. Nursing is just along for the ride.

4. The DGP is simple

Here is the data-generating process, in full. No hidden complexity — just a two-variable truth.

\[ U \in \{3, 8\} \text{ (severity)}, \qquad \Pr(U = 3) = \Pr(U = 8) = \tfrac{1}{2} \] \[ X \;=\; 2 \;+\; 0.8\,U \;+\; \eta, \qquad \eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; 0.8^2) \] \[ Y \;=\; 5 \;-\; 0.5\,X \;+\; 2\,U \;+\; \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; 0.5^2) \]

Half the patients are mild (U = 3) and half are severe (U = 8) — a two-point severity distribution. This clean split is what makes the within-group structure visible in the scatter above: each "cloud" of dots corresponds to one severity level, with X varying only through the nursing-assignment noise η.

The coefficient on X is −0.5. Negative. Nursing helps. Simpson's paradox is the catastrophe of aggregating over a missing variable.

5. The OVB formula explains exactly

The sign flip is not mysterious. It is arithmetic. Before we write it down, let's name each term carefully — the formula looks innocent, but every symbol carries weight.

  • \(\operatorname*{plim}\hat\beta^{\text{short}}\) — the probability limit of the estimator: the value \(\hat\beta^{\text{short}}\) converges to as the sample size grows to infinity. Think of it as "what the short regression would report if we had infinite data."
  • \(\hat\beta^{\text{short}}\) — the OLS estimate of the slope on X when we fit \(Y\) on \(X\) alone, ignoring the confounder U. This is what Dr. Naive reports.
  • \(\beta_X = -0.5\) — the true causal effect of nursing on recovery, read directly off the DGP. (Nursing helps.)
  • \(\beta_U = +2\) — the true direct effect of severity on recovery, also from the DGP. (Severity hurts — a lot.)
  • \(\delta \;=\; \dfrac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, U)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)}\) — the auxiliary slope: the coefficient you would get from regressing the omitted variable U on the included variable X. It measures how strongly X tracks U.

The omitted-variable-bias formula then says:

\[ \operatorname*{plim}\hat\beta^{\text{short}} \;=\; \beta_X \;+\; \underbrace{\beta_U \cdot \delta}_{\text{bias}} \]

In plain words: the bias the naive regression suffers is the true effect of the omitted variable, times how strongly X correlates with it. If either factor is zero, the bias vanishes.

Plug in the Nursing DGP. With U taking values 3 and 8 with equal probability, \(\operatorname{Var}(U) = 6.25\), so \(\operatorname{Cov}(X, U) = 0.8 \cdot 6.25 = 5.00\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(X) = 0.8^2 \cdot 6.25 + 0.8^2 = 4.64\). Hence:

\[ \delta \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, U)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)} \;=\; \frac{5.00}{4.64} \;\approx\; 1.08 \] \[ \operatorname*{plim}\hat\beta^{\text{short}} \;=\; \beta_X + \beta_U \cdot \delta \;=\; -0.5 \;+\; 2 \cdot 1.08 \;\approx\; +1.66 \]

Severity drives nursing hours almost one-for-one (\(\delta \approx 1.08\)), and severity's direct effect on recovery (\(\beta_U = +2\)) is four times larger than nursing's (\(\beta_X = -0.5\)). The larger effect wins; the bias swamps the truth; the sign flips. In our finite sample of 40 patients we happen to see \(\hat\beta^{\text{short}} \approx +1.44\) — close to the plim, with the gap due to sampling noise.

6. Try it yourself

The strength of the confounding depends on how strongly severity drives nursing hours. Call this \(\gamma\) — the coefficient on \(U\) in the equation for \(X\). In the real DGP, \(\gamma = 0.8\). What if the hospital assigned nursing without regard to severity (\(\gamma = 0\))? What if the link were even stronger?

7. The real-world corollary

The canonical real example is the 1973 UC Berkeley graduate admissions case. Aggregated across the university, men were admitted at higher rates than women. Prima facie evidence of sex discrimination?

Not quite. Within each individual department, women were admitted at equal or higher rates than men. Women simply applied in greater numbers to more competitive departments (lower admission rates overall), while men applied more to less competitive departments. Department was the confounder. Include it, and the pooled sign flips — just like in the nursing paradox.

Every time you read a surprising sign in an observational study — "exercise is bad for you," "more inspections cause more defects," "richer countries consume less energy" — ask: what confounder could be doing the work?
