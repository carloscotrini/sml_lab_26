Issue 03 · The Wrong Shape

Dr. Bishop and the polynomial abyss

If the true curve isn't a line, a straight-line fit is wrong. But giving yourself "enough flexibility" is a trap with teeth.

Section 1A new researcher enters

Dr. Bishop

"Dr. Gauss assumed the relationship is linear. What if it's curved? What if the first few lectures help a lot and later ones level off? Let me try polynomial regression."

Dr. Bishop arrives after Dr. Gauss has already fit a straight line to Y vs. X₂. She is unconvinced. Nothing in nature is truly linear; why should study outcomes be? She opens her notebook and writes down a plan: polynomial regression — same OLS machinery, but on curved features.

Section 2Why X₂, and why polynomials?

Bishop doesn't pick X₂ (lectures attended) at random. She has reasons:

  1. X₂'s noise is homoscedastic. In this DGP the noise term ε has variance proportional to |X₁|, not to X₂. Looking at Y as a function of X₂, the scatter around the true curve has roughly the same spread everywhere — a much cleaner setting to see the shape of a fitted curve.
  2. Polynomials are universal approximators. The Stone–Weierstrass theorem says, informally: any continuous function on a bounded interval can be approximated arbitrarily well by a polynomial of high enough degree. So polynomials are a very expressive tool — in principle, they can recover any smooth shape.
  3. Small, clean dataset. Bishop deliberately uses only 10 students. With a handful of points the underfit, good-fit, and overfit regimes are easy to see.
Misspecification vs. omitted variables. OVB is about a missing variable. Misspecification is about a wrong shape. You can have one without the other — and either can wreck your coefficients.

Section 3Ten students

Here is the sample Bishop draws from the shared exam-score DGP — ten students, plotted as Y (exam score) against X₂ (lectures attended).

Ten blue dots. Not much, but enough to see the story.

Section 4A polynomial is just a linear model in disguise

The key — and often surprising — teaching point:

Polynomial regression is just linear regression with transformed features.

Bishop writes the model

\[ \hat{Y} \;=\; a_0 \;+\; a_1 X_2 \;+\; a_2 X_2^2 \;+\; a_3 X_2^3 \;+\; \ldots \;+\; a_d X_2^d. \]

This has \(d+1\) coefficients. It is linear in the coefficients \((a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_d)\), even though it is nonlinear in \(X_2\). So you can fit it with ordinary least squares: build a design matrix whose columns are

\[ \big[\, 1,\; X_2,\; X_2^2,\; X_2^3,\; \ldots,\; X_2^d \,\big] \]

and run the same OLS you already know. Nothing new under the hood — just fancier columns.

The faint grey dots are the test set: 400 fresh students from the same DGP. The test \(R^2\) is computed on these out-of-sample points. When the polynomial wiggles to pass through the blue training dots but misses the grey cloud, that's overfitting made visible.

Section 6What the slider tells you

  • d = 0 — a flat line. The model can't tilt. "Too rigid to capture anything," Bishop says. Underfit.
  • d = 1 — a straight line. This is exactly Dr. Gauss's Y vs. X₂ fit. A reasonable approximation for the range of lectures in the sample.
  • d = 2–3 — a gentle curve. Training and test error both low. The sweet spot.
  • d = 5–7 — the curve starts to wiggle, chasing individual points. Training error shrinks; test error begins to rise.
  • d = 9 — with 10 data points and 10 coefficients, the polynomial passes exactly through every training point. Training MSE is essentially zero. The curve between points swings wildly. Test error explodes.

This is Bishop's PRML Figure 1.4, live. A high-degree polynomial has "memorized" the training set — including its noise. Between points it has no reason to behave, and it doesn't.

Section 7The lesson

Polynomials are universal approximators. That is a double-edged sword.

Enough flexibility to capture any shape, and enough flexibility to memorize noise. The right degree is neither the lowest (underfit) nor the highest (overfit) — it is the one that generalizes: low error on data you haven't seen.

Two notes before Bishop closes her notebook:

  • Misspecification is not OVB. OVB is a missing variable (the ghost U); misspecification is a wrong shape. Either one can make your coefficients lie. You can also have both at once.
  • The fix isn't "always use a bigger model." More flexibility without more data is how you get the wild curves at d = 9. The proper tools — cross-validation, regularization, held-out test sets — are waiting for you on the next card.

Where does Dr. Bishop go next?

What you just watched at d = 9 has a name: overfitting. The same pattern — training error falling to zero while test error explodes — is one face of the bias–variance tradeoff.
