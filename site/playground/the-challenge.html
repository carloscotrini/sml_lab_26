Between the world and the estimator

What are we actually trying to do?

A leitmotif for every page that follows.

You just built a world — a whole data-generating process, variable by variable, noise-term by noise-term. Now pause. In real life, nobody hands us that world on a silver platter. We don't know U. We don't know the true coefficients. We don't even know whether \(Y\) depends on \(X_1\) linearly, logarithmically, or not at all. We only see a table of numbers, and we have to work backwards.

This is what nature gives us — and this is what we actually get

The picture below separates the two:

Two worlds, one arrow. On the left is the machine that generates the numbers. Only nature gets to see it. On the right is the spreadsheet we actually wake up to — four columns, 150 rows, no labels telling us which terms are log and which are linear. The whole remaining course is about the arrow: how do we get from the table back to a working model, knowing the truth is permanently out of reach?

Three new students arrive

To make the stakes concrete: tomorrow morning, three new students walk in. The university has their hours-studied and lectures-attended records. They haven't taken the exam yet. The dean wants a prediction for each — a best guess at the score they'll get.

Notice: for each new student we still only see \(X_1\) and \(X_2\). Their self-discipline is invisible. Their exam-day noise hasn't happened yet. The question on the table is: given only the 150 rows of past data, what is our best guess for the Y of each of these three?

This is the leitmotif

Every page after this one is about solving a version of this puzzle. Some will be about finding the right function (so the prediction hits the target). Others will be about trusting our answer (how confident should we be?). A few will be about spectacular ways the inference can go wrong.

The DGP we just built is a teaching scaffold: it lets us know, in this notebook, what the "true" answer is, so we can catch our methods red-handed when they stray. In any real study you never get that luxury. You get the table. You get to pick a model family. You get to fit it. And then you get to wonder whether your fit is telling you something about the world or something about your sample.
