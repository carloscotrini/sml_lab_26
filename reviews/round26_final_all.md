# Round 26 -- Final Verdicts

## Max (Bored Pragmatist) -- Final rating: 9/10

The coefficient tracker running through all nine versions of the same estimate is the single best pedagogical device I have seen in a stats course, and the "Statistical BS Detector" cheat sheet alone is worth the price of admission -- I would actually pin that to my wall, which is more than I can say for any other appendix I have ever read.

## Sofia (Social Butterfly) -- Final rating: 9/10

The Pete Davidson collider example, the Ofqual cold open, and the "Is this a seat number?" catchphrase give me things I can actually explain at dinner, and the Toy Stories (darts, movie star spouses, the kid who scored 69 vs 70) make every section feel like a conversation rather than a textbook -- I genuinely want to discuss these with people.

## Lena (Anxious Perfectionist) -- Final rating: 9/10

Every theorem has a proof in the appendix, every numerical example is hand-verifiable, the notation table is complete, the two-pass reading guide removes all ambiguity about what to study when, and the practice problems with full answer key close the last gap I could find -- my only lingering worry is that the FWL numerical example could show one more intermediate calculation, but that is a nitpick unworthy of a full point.

## Niklas (Skeptical Contrarian) -- Final rating: 8/10

I concede: the notes earn their swagger -- the running Ofqual example pays off in the conclusion, the IV and RDD sections do not oversell their fixes, the sensitivity analysis section honestly admits what cannot be tested, and the repeated warning that diagnostics check the model but not the design is exactly the kind of epistemic humility most textbooks lack -- but I withhold the last two points because the DGP is still a single synthetic world and real messy data would stress-test these lessons harder.

## Fresh Eyes (Science Communicator) -- Final verdict: "excellent"

These notes accomplish something rare -- they build a complete mathematical framework (OVB through RDD, with proofs) while remaining genuinely enjoyable to read, using a single evolving story and a ghost metaphor that turns abstract bias into something you can feel -- they fall just short of "legendary" only because the real-data section leans on the Lalonde description rather than walking the reader through actual numbers, but as a self-contained document that could convert a stats-skeptic into a careful practitioner, this is outstanding work.
